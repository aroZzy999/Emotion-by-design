Emotion, By Design

It's not just you — the space is shaping your feelings.

By Ziyu Zhang

You thought it was just a space — neutral, silent, passive.

But what if the walls, the air, the lights around you were silently tuning your mind, moment by moment?

This isn’t a design fiction. It’s happening now — in classrooms, in offices, in your living room. The invisible patterns of your environment are quietly scripting your emotions.



We should treat emotional design in environmental variables as seriously as we treat concrete.

What Even Is "Invisible Space"?

Invisible space isn’t empty. It’s full of influence.

Light levels. Air movement. Subtle sounds. They shape how we feel, how we behave, even how we think.

Most people never notice. And that’s the power of it. If you can’t see it — you can’t resist it. But once you do see it, you start to feel it.

It’s completely silent — and still draining you.

CO₂ buildup makes you drowsy, dulls your attention, and slows mental performance — all without a sound. Stale air silently suffocates your focus, even when everything seems still.

You think it’s quiet. But your body disagrees.

SOUND levels above 45 dB trigger micro-stress, even if you’ve stopped noticing them. Your body stays alert, your mind keeps bracing. Fatigue follows.

Your body isn’t complaining — it’s whispering.

TEMPERATURE mismatches — even when subtle — make you fidget, lose focus, and feel uneasy. You may not say “it’s too warm,” but your body already knows.

Too much of a good thing can make you tired.

LUX — high levels energise, but excessive brightness overwhelms the senses. Light designed to help you work might also prevent you from winding down.

These aren’t just environmental details — they are emotional levers.

Once you see them, you start to feel them.

The Research Behind the Mood

These emotional responses aren’t imagined — they’re measurable.

For decades, scientists and building standards have warned us that poor air quality, unbalanced lighting, disruptive noise, and thermal discomfort can significantly affect how we feel, think, and function.

What you see throughout this site — the changes in mood, the color of boxes, the emotional rhythms — are not just speculative. They are drawn directly from how the body responds to its surroundings.

When Rhythms Don’t Align



Our bodies follow a natural rhythm—but our spaces often don’t. We thrive when our environment flows with us: clarity in the morning, calm in the afternoon, and dimness at night. When light stays too bright, or CO₂ and noise levels peak at the wrong time, even subtle mismatches can disrupt our focus and mood. This section visualizes that misalignment using a "Mismatch Score"—a simple way to show how far a space strays from what your body expects at each hour.

Your body already knows the rhythm.


Just like sunlight fades and our mind calms, our body thrives on subtle daily patterns — alert in the morning, relaxed at night. This black line shows the ideal rhythm your body expects.

Some zones ignore your rhythm completely.


Zone 243 stays constantly high across the day — meaning persistent mismatch. Maybe the air is stale, the noise level is too high, or the lighting never softens. This isn’t just uncomfortable — it can be emotionally exhausting.

Mismatch isn’t always what you think.


Zone 27 dips dramatically in the middle of the day — just when you’re supposed to feel most alert. It’s not that your energy dropped. It’s that the environment failed to support it.

While each emotion is linked to specific environmental values (like CO₂, Lux, SPL), misaligned rhythms alone can also trigger these feelings. Even when average conditions seem “normal,” the way these variables fluctuate throughout the day — either too fast, too slow, or too flat — can deeply affect how we feel.



Sudden and frequent spikes in environmental rhythm create overstimulation.

Flat, unchanging rhythms disconnect people from their surroundings.

Long hours of low activity and no rhythm cause emotional numbness.

Natural, moderate rhythms support emotional stability and focus.

Slight but constant fluctuations prevent the body from settling.

What Can We Do About It

We don’t always get to choose our environments. Most of us can’t redesign our classrooms or office lighting. But we can start noticing. And once we notice, we can shift what we can control. Whether you're a designer, policymaker, or just someone trying to concentrate, this project is an invitation to pay attention. Space isn’t neutral. Sometimes it shouts. Sometimes it whispers. And if we listen — really listen — we can start to make changes that matter. Not all change has to be big. But awareness? That’s always powerful.
